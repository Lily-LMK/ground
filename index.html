Practice guide

Do breath-work with a straight spine, sitting or lying comfortably. Practise when you first wake, before bed, and as the need arises.

Observe — the most subtle and powerful practice. Follow your breath without trying to influence it. This is the essence of meditation. Start by noticing the exhale first; you will naturally deepen your breathing by increasing exhalation.

4-7-8 — the relaxing breath. Begin with 4 cycles, twice a day. Over weeks, build to 8 cycles, twice a day. Use when anxious, upset, or experiencing cravings.

Bhastrika — the bellows breath, a stimulating exercise. Use to increase alertness when drowsy or to sharpen focus. Work up to one full minute.

Coherent — the surrender breath. Use when falling asleep or waking up. Lie down and feel yourself as the passive recipient of breath. As you inhale, the universe blows breath into you, expanding into every part of your body, even your fingers and toes. As you exhale, the universe withdraws air from you — you are not pushing it out, it is being gently taken.

To prepare for meditation: Bhastrika for 1 minute, followed by 8 cycles of 4-7-8.

When upset: Make your breath deeper, quieter, slower, and more regular. When agitated, breathing is rapid, shallow, irregular, and noisy. You cannot always tell yourself not to be upset, but you can control your breathing.

Apnea training — CO₂ tables hold your breath at a fixed time while shortening rest intervals, training CO₂ tolerance. O₂ tables keep rest fixed while lengthening hold time, training oxygen efficiency. Set your current max hold and the app generates 8 rounds. During the breathe phase, breathe slowly and calmly through the nose — belly rises on inhale, falls on exhale. No deep gulps, no fast breathing. Just tidal breath, as though you were about to fall asleep. A chime will warn you three seconds before the hold begins. Take one final full breath, then hold. Tap to release when you need to breathe. Always train lying down, never in water alone.

Regularity matters more than intensity. Daily practice, over time, changes the function of the nervous system.
